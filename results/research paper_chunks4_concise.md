# 1. Paper Overview
This study explores the intersection of learner autonomy and mobile-assisted language learning (MALL) among advanced English as a Foreign Language (EFL) students. It investigates how these learners utilize mobile technology to support their language goals, needs, and personal learning styles, while situating these findings within theoretical frameworks regarding autonomy in second and foreign language education.

# 2. Problem Statement
The research addresses the need to align educational technology with the realities of learner autonomy and the students' specific learning contexts. While mobile devices play an increasing role in formal and informal learning, there is a gap in understanding how advanced learners effectively integrate these tools to foster self-directed learning.

# 3. Proposed Method
The study employs a qualitative and quantitative analysis of semi-structured interview data gathered from 20 advanced EFL students. The research design incorporates a literature review on learner autonomy—referencing theorists such as Henri Holec—and examines how mobile device affordances (smartphones and tablets) facilitate technology-mediated learner autonomy.

# 4. Data and Experimental Setup
*   **Participants:** 20 advanced EFL students.
*   **Data Collection:** Semi-structured interviews.
*   **Analysis:** A combined qualitative and quantitative approach was used to interpret how participants utilize mobile devices for language learning.

# 5. Main Results
*   **High Awareness:** Learners exhibit a strong awareness of how mobile devices benefit customized learning and the achievement of language goals.
*   **Ad Hoc Usage:** Despite this awareness, the actual application of mobile devices within classroom settings was observed to be largely intuitive or ad hoc rather than systematic.
*   **Nature of Autonomy:** Research indicates that learners possess an innate tendency toward control, that autonomy is a multidimensional concept influenced by social and contextual factors, and that autonomous learning is generally more effective than non-autonomous approaches.

# 6. Strengths
*   Provides a multidimensional theoretical framework for understanding learner autonomy.
*   Connects abstract concepts of self-directed learning with practical, modern technology use.
*   Combines both qualitative and quantitative data collection methods to provide a broader view of student behavior.
